Peripancreatic fluid collections
  • Occur in 25% cases<\/li>
  • Located in or near the pancreas and lack a wall of granulation or fibrous tissue<\/li>
  • May resolve or develop into pseudocysts or abscesses<\/li>
  • Since most resolve aspiration and drainage is best avoided as it may precipitate infection<\/li><\/ul>
    Pseudocysts
    • result from the organisation of a peripancreatic fluid collection<\/span>. They may or may not communicate with the ductal system.<\/li>
    • The collection is walled by fibrous or granulation tissue<\/span> and typically occur 2-4 weeks or more after an attack of acute pancreatitis<\/li>
    • Most are retrogastric<\/li>
    • 75% are associated with persistent mild elevation of amylase<\/li>
    • Investigation is with CT, ERCP and MRI or endoscopic USS<\/li>
    • Symptomatic cases may be observed for 12 weeks as up to 50% resolve<\/span><\/li>
    • Treatment is either with endoscopic or surgical cystogastrostomy or aspiration<\/li><\/ul>
      Pancreatic necrosis
      • Pancreatic necrosis may involve both the pancreatic parenchyma and surrounding fat<\/li>
      • Complications are directly linked to extent of parenchymal necrosis and extent of necrosis overall<\/li>
      • Early necrosectomy is associated with a high mortality rate (and should be avoided unless compelling indications for surgery exist)<\/li>
      • Sterile necrosis should be managed conservatively (at least initially)<\/li>
      • Some centres will perform fine-needle aspiration sampling of necrotic tissue if infection is suspected. False negatives may occur and the extent of sepsis and organ dysfunction may be a better guide to surgery<\/li><\/ul>
        Pancreatic abscess
        • Intraabdominal collection of pus associated with pancreas but in the absence of necrosis<\/li>
        • Typically occur as a result of infected pseudocyst<\/li>
        • Transgastric drainage is one method of treatment, endoscopic drainage is an alternative<\/li><\/ul>
          Haemorrhage
          • Infected necrosis may involve vascular structures with resultant haemorrhage that may occur de novo or as a result of surgical necrosectomy.<\/li>
          • When retroperitoneal haemorrhage occurs Grey Turner's sign may be identified<\/li><\/ul>

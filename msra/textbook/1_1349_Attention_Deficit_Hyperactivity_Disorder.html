    Management<\/h5>
    NICE stipulates a holistic approach to treating ADHD that isn't entirely reliant on therapeutics. Following presentation, a ten-week 'watch and wait' period should follow to observe whether symptoms change or resolve. If they persist then referral to secondary care is required. This is normally to a paediatrician with a special interest in behavioural disorders, or to the local Child and Adolescent Mental Health Service (CAMHS). Here, the needs and wants of the patient, as well as how their condition affects their lives should be taken into account, to offer a tailored plan of action.

    Drug therapy should be seen as a last resort<\/b> and is only available to those aged 5 years or more<\/b>. Patients with mild\/moderate symptoms can usually benefit from their parents attending education and training programmes. For those who fail to respond, or whose symptoms are severe, pharmacotherapy can be considered:
    • Methylphenidate<\/span> is first line in children<\/b> and should initially be given on a six-week trial basis. It is a CNS stimulant which primarily acts as a dopamine\/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor<\/span>. Side-effects include abdominal pain, nausea and dyspepsia<\/span>. In children, weight and height should be monitored every 6 months<\/span><\/li>
    • If there is inadequate response, switch to lisdexamfetamine;<\/li>
    • Dexamfetamine should be started in those who have benefited from lisdexamfetamine, but who can't tolerate its side effects.<\/li><\/ul>
      In adults:
      • Methylphenidate or lisdexamfetamine are first-line options;<\/li>
      • Switch between these drugs if no benefit is seen after a trial of the other.<\/li><\/ul>
        All of these drugs are potentially cardiotoxic<\/b>. Perform a baseline ECG before starting treatment, and refer to a cardiologist if there is any significant past medical history or family history, or any doubt or ambiguity.

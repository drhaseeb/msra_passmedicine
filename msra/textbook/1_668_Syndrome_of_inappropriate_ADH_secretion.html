[{"notes_id":"1_668","category":"3","subcategory":"2","title":"Syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion","body":"The syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion (SIADH) is characterised by hyponatraemia secondary to the dilutional effects of excessive water retention. Patients with SIADH are typically euvolemic, i.e. they do not show signs of overt volume depletion or overload.

Pathophysiology
  • SIADH involves an excessive release of antidiuretic hormone (ADH), also known as vasopressin, which leads to water retention, volume expansion, and dilutional hyponatraemia<\/li>
  • ADH is produced by the hypothalamus and stored in the posterior pituitary gland. Its primary function is to regulate the body's water balance<\/li>
  • It does this by increasing water reabsorption in the collecting ducts of the kidneys, thereby decreasing the volume of urine produced<\/li>
  • In SIADH, there is an inappropriate and continuous release of ADH that is not inhibited by normal physiological mechanisms, such as adequate or excess body fluid levels<\/li>
  • As a result, the kidneys reabsorb more water, leading to decreased urine output, and expansion of extracellular fluid volume.<\/li>
  • Importantly, this increase in body fluid volume does not lead to the expected signs of fluid overload, such as oedema or hypertension, because the excess fluid is uniformly distributed throughout all body fluid compartments.<\/li>
  • However, as water is retained in the body, the concentration of electrolytes in the blood, particularly sodium, becomes diluted, leading to hyponatraemia. <\/li><\/ul>

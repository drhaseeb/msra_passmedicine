[{"notes_id":"1_1439","category":"7","subcategory":"0","title":"Scarlet fever","body":"Scarlet fever is a reaction to erythrogenic toxins produced by Group A haemolytic streptococci (usually Streptococcus pyogenes<\/i>). It is more common in children aged 2 - 6 years with the peak incidence being at 4 years.

Scarlet fever is spread via the respiratory route by inhaling or ingesting respiratory droplets or by direct contact with nose and throat discharges, (especially during sneezing and coughing).

Scarlet fever has an incubation period of 2-4 days and typically presents with<\/span>:
  • fever: typically lasts 24 to 48 hours<\/li>
  • malaise, headache, nausea\/vomiting<\/li>
  • sore throat<\/li>
  • 'strawberry' tongue<\/li>
  • rash
    • fine punctate erythema ('pinhead') which generally appears first on the torso and spares the palms and soles<\/li>
    • children often have a flushed appearance with circumoral pallor. The rash is often more obvious in the flexures<\/li>
    • it is often described as having a rough 'sandpaper' texture<\/span><\/li>
    • desquamination occurs later in the course of the illness, particularly around the fingers and toes<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>

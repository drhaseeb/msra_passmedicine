Cross-matching<\/h5>
Key points
  • ABO compatibility is critical - Transfusing ABO-incompatible red cells can cause rapid, life-threatening haemolysis<\/li>
  • RhD compatibility matters - RhD-negative patients should receive RhD-negative red cells to prevent alloimmunisation, especially in women of childbearing age<\/li>
  • group O red cells have no A or B antigens - Making O the universal donor group for RBCs in emergencies<\/li>
  • group AB individuals have no anti-A or anti-B antibodies - Making them universal recipients for red cell transfusions<\/li>
  • antibodies are in the plasma, antigens are on the red cells - Matching is based on preventing recipient antibodies from attacking donor red cell antigens<\/li>
  • alloantibodies can develop after prior transfusion or pregnancy - Extended antigen matching may be needed for patients with known antibodies<\/li>
  • emergency transfusions may use uncrossmatched O-negative blood - But group-specific, crossmatched blood should be used as soon as possible<\/span><\/li><\/ul>

Epididymal cysts<\/h5>
Epididymal cysts are the most common cause of scrotal swellings seen in primary care.

Features
  • separate from the body of the testicle<\/li>
  • found posterior to the testicle<\/li><\/ul>
    Associated conditions
    • polycystic kidney disease <\/li>
    • cystic fibrosis<\/li>
    • von Hippel-Lindau syndrome<\/li><\/ul>
      Diagnosis may be confirmed by ultrasound.

      Management is usually supportive but surgical removal or sclerotherapy may be attempted for larger or symptomatic cysts.

      Hydrocele<\/h5>
      A hydrocele describes the accumulation of fluid within the tunica vaginalis. They can be divided into communicating and non-communicating:
      • communicating: caused by patency of the processus vaginalis allowing peritoneal fluid to drain down into the scrotum. Communicating hydroceles are common in newborn males (clinically apparent in 5-10%) and usually resolve within the first few months of life<\/li>
      • non-communicating: caused by excessive fluid production within the tunica vaginalis<\/li><\/ul>
        Hydroceles may develop secondary to:
        • epididymo-orchitis<\/li>
        • testicular torsion<\/li>
        • testicular tumours<\/li><\/ul>
          Features
          • soft, non-tender swelling of the hemi-scrotum. Usually anterior to and below the testicle<\/li>
          • the swelling is confined to the scrotum, you can get 'above' the mass on examination<\/li>
          • transilluminates with a pen torch<\/li>
          • the testis may be difficult to palpate if the hydrocele is large<\/li><\/ul>
            Diagnosis may be clinical but ultrasound is required if there is any doubt about the diagnosis or if the underlying testis cannot be palpated.

            Management
            • infantile hydroceles are generally repaired if they do not resolve spontaneously by the age of 1-2 years<\/li>
            • in adults a conservative approach may be taken depending on the severity of the presentation. Further investigation (e.g. ultrasound) is usually warranted however to exclude any underlying cause such as a tumour<\/li><\/ul>
              Varicocele<\/h5>
              A varicocele is an abnormal enlargement of the testicular veins. They are usually asymptomatic but may be important as they are associated with infertility.

              Varicoceles are much more common on the left side (> 80%). Features:
              • classically described as a 'bag of worms'<\/li>
              • subfertility<\/li><\/ul>
                Diagnosis
                • ultrasound with Doppler studies<\/li><\/ul>
                  Management
                  • usually conservative<\/li>

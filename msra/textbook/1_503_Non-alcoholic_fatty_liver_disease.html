  • steatosis - fat in the liver<\/li>
  • steatohepatitis - fat with inflammation, non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), see below<\/li>
  • progressive disease may cause fibrosis and liver cirrhosis<\/li><\/ul>
    NAFLD is thought to represent the hepatic manifestation of the metabolic syndrome and hence insulin resistance<\/span> is thought to be the key mechanism leading to steatosis.

    Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) is a term used to describe liver changes similar to those seen in alcoholic hepatitis in the absence of a history of alcohol abuse. It is relatively common and thought to affect around 3-4% of the general population. The progression of disease in patients with NASH may be responsible for a proportion of patients previously labelled as cryptogenic cirrhosis.

    Associated factors
    • obesity<\/span><\/li>
    • type 2 diabetes mellitus<\/span><\/li>
    • hyperlipidaemia<\/li>
    • jejunoileal bypass<\/li>
    • sudden weight loss\/starvation<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
      Features
      • usually asymptomatic<\/li>
      • hepatomegaly<\/li>
      • ALT is typically greater than AST<\/span><\/li>
      • increased echogenicity on ultrasound<\/li><\/ul>
        NICE produced guidelines on the investigation and management of NAFLD in 2016. Key points:
        • there is no evidence to support screening for NAFLD in adults, even in at risk groups (e.g. type 2 diabetes)<\/li>
        • the guidelines are therefore based on the management of the incidental<\/i> finding of NAFLD - typically asymptomatic fatty changes on liver ultrasound<\/li>
        • in these patients, NICE recommends the use of the enhanced liver fibrosis (ELF)<\/span> blood test to check for advanced fibrosis<\/li>
        • the ELF blood test is a combination of hyaluronic acid + procollagen III + tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase 1. An algorithm based on these values results in an ELF blood test score, similar to triple testing for Down's syndrome<\/li><\/ul>
          An excellent review by Byrne et Al1<\/sup> in 2018 reviewed the diagnosis and monitoring of NAFLD. It made the following suggestions if the ELF blood test was not available:
          • non-invasive tests may be used to assess the severity of fibrosis<\/li>
          • these include the FIB4 score or NALFD fibrosis score<\/li>
          • these scores may be used in combination with a FibroScan (liver stiffness measurement assessed with transient elastography)<\/li>
          • this combination has been shown to have excellent accuracy in predicting fibrosis<\/li><\/ul>
            Patients who are likely to have advanced fibrosis should be referred to a liver specialist. They will then likely have a liver biopsy to stage the disease more accurately.

            Management
            • the mainstay of treatment is lifestyle changes (particularly weight loss<\/span>) and monitoring<\/li>
            • there is ongoing research into the role of gastric banding and insulin-sensitising drugs (e.g. metformin, pioglitazone<\/span>)<\/li><\/ul>

The updated guidelines put less emphasis on the size of the pneumothorax and more emphasis on whether the patient is symptomatic and the presence of high-risk characteristics.

Decision algorithm<\/h5>
The first step is assessing whether the patient is symptomatic
  • the BTS define minimal symptoms as 'no significant pain or breathlessness and no physiological compromise<\/i>'<\/li>
  • no or minimal symptoms → conservative care, regardless of pneumothorax size<\/span><\/li>
  • symptomatic → assess for high-risk characteristics<\/li><\/ul>
    If a pneumothorax is symptomatic, the next step is assessment for high-risk characteristics
    • high-risk characteristics<\/span> are defined as follows:
      • haemodynamic compromise (suggesting a tension pneumothorax)<\/li>
      • significant hypoxia<\/li>
      • bilateral pneumothorax<\/li>
      • underlying lung disease<\/li>
      • \u2265 50 years of age with significant smoking history<\/li>
      • haemothorax<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
      • if no high-risk characteristics are present, and it is safe to intervene, then there is a choice of intervention<\/span>:
        • conservative care<\/li>
        • ambulatory device<\/li>
        • needle aspiration<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
        • if high-risk characteristics are present, and it is safe to intervene → chest drain<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
          How is safety or intervention<\/span> determined?
          • before a needle aspiration\/chest drain insertion, the safety of intervention should be assessed<\/li>
          • this depends on the clinical context, but is usually:
            • 2cm laterally or apically on chest x-ray, or <\/li>
            • any size on CT scan which can be safely accessed with radiological support<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>

              Management options<\/h5>
              Conservative care
              • patients with a primary spontaneous pneumothorax that is managed conservatively should be reviewed every 2-4 days as an outpatient<\/span> <\/li>
              • patients with a secondary spontaneous pneumothorax that is managed conservatively should be monitored as an inpatient<\/span> <\/li>
              • if stable, follow-up in the outpatients department in 2-4 weeks<\/li><\/ul>
                Ambulatory care
                • an example of an ambulatory device is the Rocket\u00b5 Pleural Vent\u201e<\/li>
                • it includes an 8FG catheter mounted on an 18G needle and a pigtail catheter to minimize the risk of occlusion<\/li>
                • ambulatory devices typically have a one-way valve and vent to prevent air and fluid return to the pleural space while allowing for controlled escape of air and drainage of fluid<\/li>
                • many devices also have an indication diaphragm that signals when the catheter tip enters the pleural space and continues to fluctuate with respiration, aiding in the assessment of pneumothorax resolution<\/li><\/ul>
                  <\/a><\/div><\/div><\/div>
                  Needle aspiration
                  • a chest drain should be inserted if needle aspiration of a pneumothorax is unsuccessful <\/span> <\/li>
                  • if resolved, discharge and follow-up in the outpatients department in 2-4 weeks<\/li><\/ul>
                    Chest drain insertion
                    • daily review as an inpatient<\/li>
                    • remove drain when resolved<\/li>
                    • discharge and follow-up in the outpatients department in 2-4 weeks<\/li><\/ul>
                      Persistentent \/ recurrent pneumothorax<\/h5>
                      If a patient has a persistent air leak or insufficient lung reexpansion despite chest drain insertion, or the patient has recurrent pneumothoraces, then video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS)<\/span> should be considered to allow for mechanical\/chemical pleurodesis +\/- bullectomy.

                      Discharge advice<\/h5>
                      Smoking
                      • patients should be advised to avoid smoking to reduce the risk of further episodes - the lifetime risk of developing a pneumothorax in healthy smoking men is around 10% compared with around 0.1% in non-smoking men<\/li><\/ul>
                        Fitness to fly
                        • absolute contraindication, the CAA suggest patients may travel 2 weeks after successful drainage if there is no residual air. The British Thoracic Society used to recommend not travelling by air for a period of 6 weeks but this has now been changed to 1 week post check x-ray<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
                          Scuba diving<\/span>
                          • the BTS guidelines state: 'Diving should be permanently avoided unless the patient has undergone bilateral surgical pleurectomy and has normal lung function and chest CT scan postoperatively.'<\/i><\/li><\/ul>

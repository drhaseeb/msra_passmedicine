        Rate control<\/h5>
        Rate control should be offered as the first-line treatment strategy for atrial fibrillation except in people:
        • whose atrial fibrillation has a reversible cause<\/li>
        • who have heart failure thought to be primarily caused by atrial fibrillation<\/li>
        • with new-onset atrial fibrillation (< 48 hours)<\/li>
        • with atrial flutter whose condition is considered suitable for an ablation strategy to restore sinus rhythm<\/li>
        • for whom a rhythm-control strategy would be more suitable based on clinical judgement<\/li><\/ul>
          Medications<\/h6>
          Agents used to control rate in patients with atrial fibrillation
          • beta-blockers<\/span>
            • a common contraindication for beta-blockers is asthma<\/span><\/li><\/ul><\/li>
            • calcium channel blockers<\/li>
            • digoxin<\/span>
              • not considered first-line anymore as they are less effective at controlling the heart rate during exercise<\/li>
              • should only be considered if the person does no or very little physical exercise or other rate-limiting drug options are ruled out because of comorbidities <\/li>
              • may have a role if there is coexistent heart failure<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
                Rhythm control<\/h5>
                Agents used to maintain sinus rhythm in patients with a history of atrial fibrillation
                • beta-blockers<\/li>
                • dronedarone: second-line in patients following cardioversion<\/li>
                • amiodarone: particularly if coexisting heart failure <\/li><\/ul>

                  Catheter ablation<\/h5>
                  NICE recommends the use of catheter ablation for those with AF who have not responded to or wish to avoid, antiarrhythmic medication.

                  Technical aspects
                  • the aim is to ablate the faulty electrical pathways that are resulting in atrial fibrillation. This is typically due to aberrant electrical activity between the pulmonary veins and left atrium<\/span><\/li>
                  • the procedure is performed percutaneously, typically via the groin<\/li>
                  • both radiofrequency (uses heat generated from medium frequency alternating current) and cryotherapy can be used to ablate the tissue<\/li><\/ul>
                    Anticoagulation
                    • should be used 4 weeks before and during the procedure<\/li>
                    • it should be remember that catheter ablation controls the rhythm but does not reduce the stroke risk, even if patients remain in sinus rhythm. Therefore, patients still require anticoagulation as per their CHA2<\/sub>DS2<\/sub>-VASc score<\/span>
                      • if score = 0: 2 months anticoagulation recommended<\/li>
                      • if score > 1: longterm anticoagulation recommended<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>

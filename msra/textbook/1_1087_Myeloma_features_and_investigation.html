It arises due to genetic mutations which occur as B-lymphocytes differentiate into mature plasma cells.

Features<\/h5>
The median age at presentation is 70 years old.

Use the mnemonic CRABBI<\/b>:<\/span>

    • much less common contributing factors: impaired renal function, increased renal tubular calcium reabsorption and elevated PTH-rP levels<\/li>
    • this leads to constipation, nausea, anorexia and confusion<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
    • R<\/u>enal
      • monoclonal production of immunoglobulins results in light chain deposition within the renal tubules<\/span><\/li>
      • this causes renal damage which presents as dehydration and increasing thirst<\/li>
      • other causes of renal impairment in myeloma include amyloidosis, nephrocalcinosis, nephrolithiasis<\/span><\/li><\/ul><\/li>
      • A<\/u>naemia
        • bone marrow crowding suppresses erythropoiesis leading to anaemia<\/li>
        • this causes fatigue and pallor<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
        • B<\/u>leeding
          • bone marrow crowding also results in thrombocytopenia which puts patients at increased risk of bleeding and bruising<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
          • B<\/u>ones
            • bone marrow infiltration by plasma cells and cytokine-mediated osteoclast overactivity creates lytic bone lesions<\/li>
            • this may present as pain (especially in the back) and increases the risk of pathological fractures<\/span><\/li><\/ul><\/li>
            • I<\/u>nfection
              • a reduction in the production of normal immunoglobulins results in increased susceptibility to infection<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
                Other features include

                • carpal tunnel syndrome<\/li>
                • neuropathy<\/li>
                • hyperviscosity<\/li><\/ul>

                  Investigations<\/h5>
                  Bloods
                  • full blood count: anaemia<\/li>
                  • peripheral blood film: rouleaux formation<\/span><\/li>
                  • urea and electrolytes: renal failure<\/li>
                  • bone profile: hypercalcaemia<\/li>
                  • a rise in immunoglobulins may be implied by an elevated (total protein - albumin<\/span>)<\/li><\/ul>
                    Protein electrophoresis
                    • raised concentrations of monoclonal IgA\/IgG proteins will be present in the serum<\/li>
                    • in the urine, they are known as Bence Jones proteins<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
                      Bone marrow aspiration
                      • confirms the diagnosis if the number of plasma cells is significantly raised<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
                        Imaging
                        • historically a skeletal survey has been done to look for bone lesions<\/li>
                        • however, whole-body MRI<\/span> is increasingly used and is now recommended in the 2016 NICE guidelines<\/li>
                        • X-rays: 'rain-drop skull' (likened to the pattern rain forms after hitting a surface and splashing, where it leaves a random pattern of dark spots). Note that a very similar, but subtly different finding is found in primary hyperparathyroidism - 'pepperpot skull'<\/li><\/ul>

                          Diagnostic criteria<\/h5>
                          The diagnostic criteria for multiple myeloma requires one major and one minor criteria or three minor criteria in an individual who has signs or symptoms of multiple myeloma.

                          Major criteria
                          • Plasmacytoma (as demonstrated on evaluation of biopsy specimen)<\/li>
                          • 30% plasma cells in a bone marrow sample<\/li>
                          • Elevated levels of M protein in the blood or urine<\/li><\/ul>
                            Minor criteria
                            • 10% to 30% plasma cells in a bone marrow sample.<\/li>
                            • Minor elevations in the level of M protein in the blood or urine.<\/li>
                            • Osteolytic lesions (as demonstrated on imaging studies).<\/li>

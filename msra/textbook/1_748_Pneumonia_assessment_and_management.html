NICE recommends that patients should initially be assessed in primary care using the CRB65 criteria:

Patients are stratified for risk of death as follows:
  • 0: low risk (less than 1% mortality risk)
    • NICE recommend that treatment at home<\/span> should be considered (alongside clinical judgement)<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
    • 1 or 2: intermediate risk (1-10% mortality risk)
      • NICE recommend that ' hospital assessment should be considered (particularly for people with a score of 2)'<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
      • 3 or 4: high risk (more than 10% mortality risk)
        • NICE recommend urgent admission to hospital<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
          NICE also mention point-of-care CRP test. This is currently not widely available but they make the following recommendation with reference to the use of antibiotic therapy:
          • CRP < 20 mg\/L - do not routinely offer antibiotic therapy<\/li>
          • CRP 20 - 100 mg\/L - consider a delayed antibiotic prescription<\/li>
          • CRP > 100 mg\/L - offer antibiotic therapy<\/li><\/ul>

            NICE recommend, in conjunction with clinical judgement:
            • consider home-based care for patients with a CURB65 score of 0 or 1 - low risk (less than 3% mortality risk)<\/li>
            • consider hospital-based care for patients with a CURB65 score of 2 or more - intermediate risk (3-15% mortality risk)<\/li>
            • consider intensive care assessment for patients with a CURB65 score of 3 or more - high risk (more than 15% mortality risk)<\/li><\/ul>
              Investigations
              • chest x-ray<\/li>
              • in intermediate or high-risk patients NICE recommend blood and sputum cultures, pneumococcal and legionella urinary antigen tests<\/li>
              • CRP monitoring is recommend for admitted patients to help determine response to treatment<\/li><\/ul>
                Management of low-severity community acquired pneumonia
                • amoxicillin is first-line<\/span><\/li>
                • if penicillin allergic then use a macrolide or tetracycline<\/li>
                • NICE now recommend a 5 day course of antibiotics for patients with low severity community acquired pneumonia<\/li><\/ul>
                  Management of moderate and high-severity community acquired pneumonia
                  • dual antibiotic therapy is recommended with amoxicillin and a macrolide<\/li>
                  • a 7-10 day course is recommended<\/li>
                  • NICE recommend considering a beta-lactamase stable penicillin such as co-amoxiclav, ceftriaxone or piperacillin with tazobactam and a macrolide in high-severity community acquired pneumonia<\/li><\/ul>

                    Discharge criteria and advice post-discharge<\/h5>
                    NICE recommend that patients are not routinely discharged if in the past 24 hours they have had 2 or more of the following findings:
                    • temperature higher than 37.5\u00b0C<\/li>
                    • respiratory rate 24 breaths per minute or more<\/li>
                    • heart rate over 100 beats per minute<\/li>
                    • systolic blood pressure 90 mmHg or less<\/li>
                    • oxygen saturation under 90% on room air<\/li>
                    • abnormal mental status<\/li>
                    • inability to eat without assistance.<\/li><\/ul>
                      They also recommend delaying discharge if the temperature is higher than 37.5\u00b0C.

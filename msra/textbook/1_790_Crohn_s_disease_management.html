General points
  • patients should be strongly advised to stop smoking<\/li>
  • some studies suggest an increased risk of relapse secondary to NSAIDs and the combined oral contraceptive pill but the evidence is patchy<\/li><\/ul>
    Inducing remission
    • glucocorticoids (oral, topical or intravenous) are generally used to induce remission<\/span>. Budesonide is an alternative in a subgroup of patients<\/li>
    • enteral feeding with an elemental diet may be used in addition to or instead of other measures to induce remission, particularly if there is concern regarding the side-effects of steroids (for example in young children)<\/li>
    • 5-ASA drugs (e.g. mesalazine) are used second-line to glucocorticoids but are not as effective<\/li>
    • azathioprine or mercaptopurine* may be used as an add-on medication to induce remission but is not used as monotherapy. Methotrexate is an alternative to azathioprine<\/li>
    • infliximab is useful in refractory disease and fistulating Crohn's. Patients typically continue on azathioprine or methotrexate<\/li>
    • metronidazole is often used for isolated peri-anal disease<\/li><\/ul>

      • as above, stopping smoking is a priority (remember: smoking makes Crohn's worse, but may help ulcerative colitis)<\/li>

        • methotrexate is used second-line<\/li><\/ul>

                As well as the well-documented complications described above, patients are also at risk of:

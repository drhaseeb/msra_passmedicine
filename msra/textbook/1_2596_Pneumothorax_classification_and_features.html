Classification<\/h5>
Pneumothoraces can be classified as follows:
  • spontaneous pneumothorax
    • primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP)<\/b>: Occurs without underlying lung disease, often in tall, thin, young individuals. PSP is associated with the rupture of subpleural blebs or bullae.<\/li>
    • secondary spontaneous pneumothorax (SSP)<\/b>: Occurs in patients with pre-existing lung disease, such as COPD<\/span>, asthma, cystic fibrosis<\/span>, lung cancer<\/span>, Pneumocystis pneumonia. Certain connective tissue diseases such as Marfan's syndrome are also a risk factor<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
    • traumatic pneumothorax: results from penetrating or blunt chest trauma, leading to lung injury and pleural air accumulation.<\/li>
    • iatrogenic pneumothorax: occurs as a complication of medical procedures, such as thoracentesis, central venous catheter placement, ventilation<\/span>, including non-invasive ventilation<\/span> or lung biopsy.<\/li><\/ul>
      An important clinical entity is a tension pneumothorax<\/b> - this describes a severe pneumothorax resulting in the displacement of mediastinal structures that may result in severe respiratory distress and haemodynamic collapse.

      Catamenial pneumothorax<\/span> is the cause of 3-6% of spontaneous pneumothoraces occurring in menstruating women. It is thought to be caused by endometriosis within the thorax.

Basic breast anatomy<\/div>
Most breast cancers arise from duct tissue followed by lobular tissue, described as ductal<\/b> or lobular carcinoma<\/b> respectively. These can be further subdivided as to whether the cancer hasn't spread beyond the local tissue (described as carcinoma-in-situ<\/b>) or has spread (described as invasive<\/b>). Therefore, common breast cancer types include:
  • Invasive ductal carcinoma<\/b>. This is the most common type of breast cancer. To complicate matters further this has recently been renamed 'No Special Type (NST)<\/b>'. In contrast, lobular carcinoma and other rarer types of breast cancer are classified as 'Special Type'<\/li>
  • Invasive lobular carcinoma<\/b><\/li>
  • Ductal carcinoma-in-situ (DCIS)<\/b><\/li>
  • Lobular carcinoma-in-situ (LCIS)<\/b><\/li><\/ul>
    Rarer types of breast cancer are shown in the following list. These are classed as 'Special Type' but as noted previously remember that a relatively common type of breast cancer (lobular) is also Special Type:
    • Medullary breast cancer<\/li>
    • Mucinous (mucoid or colloid) breast cancer<\/li>
    • Tubular breast cancer<\/li>
    • Adenoid cystic carcinoma of the breast<\/li>
    • Metaplastic breast cancer<\/li>
    • Lymphoma of the breast<\/li>
    • Basal type breast cancer<\/li>
    • Phyllodes or cystosarcoma phyllodes<\/li>
    • Papillary breast cancer<\/li><\/ul>
      Other types of breast cancer include the following (although please note they may be associated with the underlying lesions seen above, rather than completely separate subtypes):

      Paget's disease of the nipple<\/b> is an eczematoid change of the nipple associated with an underlying breast malignancy and it is present in 1-2% of patients with breast cancer. In half of these patients, it is associated with an underlying mass lesion and 90% of such patients will have an invasive carcinoma. 30% of patients without a mass lesion will still be found to have an underlying carcinoma. The remainder will have carcinoma in situ.

      Inflammatory breast cancer<\/b> where cancerous cells block the lymph drainage resulting in an inflamed appearance of the breast.

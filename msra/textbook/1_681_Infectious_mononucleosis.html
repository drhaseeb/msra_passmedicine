Less frequent causes include cytomegalovirus and HHV-6. It is most common in adolescents and young adults.

The classic triad of sore throat, pyrexia and lymphadenopathy<\/span> is seen in around 98% of patients:
  • sore throat<\/li>
  • lymphadenopathy: may be present in the anterior and posterior triangles of the neck, in contrast to tonsillitis which typically only results in the upper anterior cervical chain being enlarged<\/li>

    • malaise, anorexia, headache<\/li>
    • palatal petechiae<\/span><\/li>
    • splenomegaly<\/span> - occurs in around 50% of patients and may rarely predispose to splenic rupture<\/li>
    • hepatitis, transient rise in ALT<\/span><\/li>
    • lymphocytosis: presence of 50% lymphocytes with at least 10% atypical lymphocytes<\/span><\/li>
    • haemolytic anaemia<\/span> secondary to cold agglutins (IgM)<\/span><\/li>

      Diagnosis
      • heterophil antibody test (Monospot test)<\/span>
        • NICE guidelines suggest FBC and Monospot in the 2nd week of the illness to confirm a diagnosis of glandular fever.<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
          Management is supportive<\/span> and includes:
          • rest during the early stages, drink plenty of fluid, avoid alcohol<\/li>

    Heparin-induced thrombocytopaenia (HIT)<\/h5>
    Immune-mediated adverse reaction to heparin causing thrombocytopenia and paradoxical thrombosis<\/span>.

    Pathophysiology
    • IgG antibodies form against platelet factor 4 (PF4)\u2013heparin complexes<\/span><\/li>
    • Antibody binding activates platelets by by cross-linking FcγIIA receptors \u2192 thrombosis and platelet consumption.<\/li><\/ul>
      Types
      • Type I: mild, non-immune, early (within 1\u20132 days), transient, not clinically significant. <\/li>
      • Type II: immune-mediated, typically 5\u201314 days after exposure, associated with thrombosis.<\/li><\/ul>
        Clinical features
        • Platelet count fall >50% from baseline. <\/li>
        • Occurs 5\u201314 days after starting heparin (earlier if previously exposed). <\/li>
        • Venous and arterial thromboses (DVT, PE, limb ischaemia, MI, stroke). <\/li>
        • Skin necrosis at injection sites.<\/li><\/ul>
          Investigations
          • 4T score to assess probability<\/span>. <\/li>
          • Confirm with HIT antibody (anti-PF4 ELISA) \u00b1 functional assays (serotonin release assay).<\/li><\/ul>
            Management
            • Stop all heparin immediately (including flushes). <\/li>
            • Avoid LMWH as cross-reactivity occurs. <\/li>
            • Start alternative anticoagulant. Options include:
              • Direct thrombin inhibitors: e.g. argatroban<\/span>, bivalirudin (particularly useful if renal impairment or rapid reversal needed)<\/li>
              • Danaparoid<\/li>
              • Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs)<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
              • Do not transfuse platelets unless life-threatening bleeding. <\/li>
              • Document allergy and avoid future heparin exposure.<\/li><\/ul>

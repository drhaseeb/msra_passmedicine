The virus characteristically affects the temporal lobes<\/span> - questions may give the result of imaging or describe temporal lobe signs e.g. aphasia.

Features<\/span>
  • fever, headache, psychiatric symptoms, seizures, vomiting<\/li>
  • focal features e.g. aphasia<\/li>
  • peripheral lesions (e.g. cold sores) have no relation to the presence of HSV encephalitis<\/li><\/ul>
    Pathophysiology
    • HSV-1 is responsible for 95% of cases in adults<\/li>
    • typically affects temporal and inferior frontal lobes<\/li><\/ul>
      Investigation
      • CSF: lymphocytosis, elevated protein<\/span><\/li>
      • PCR for HSV<\/li>
      • CT: medial temporal and inferior frontal changes (e.g. petechial haemorrhages) - normal in one-third of patients<\/li>
      • MRI is better<\/li>
      • EEG pattern<\/span>
        • unilateral or bilateral periodic sharp-and-slow-wave complexes at 2\u20133 Hz over the temporal lobes (periodic lateralised epileptiform discharges)<\/li>
        • seen in ~80% of cases<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
          Treatment
          • intravenous aciclovir<\/li><\/ul>
            The prognosis is dependent on whether aciclovir is commenced early. If treatment is started promptly the mortality is 10-20%. Left untreated the mortality approaches 80%

[{"notes_id":"1_708","category":"7","subcategory":"0","title":"Whooping cough (pertussis)","body":"Whooping cough (pertussis) is an infectious disease caused by the Gram-negative bacterium Bordetella pertussis<\/i>. It typically presents in children. There are around 1,000 cases are reported each year in the UK. It is sometimes called the 'cough of 100 days'.

Immunisation
  • infants are routinely immunised at 2, 3, 4 months and 3-5 years. Newborn infants are particularly vulnerable, which is why the vaccination campaign for pregnant women was introduced<\/li>
  • neither infection nor immunisation results in lifelong protection - hence adolescents and adults may develop whooping cough despite having had their routine immunisations<\/li><\/ul>
    Features<\/span>
    • catarrhal phase
      • symptoms are similar to a viral upper respiratory tract infection<\/li>
      • lasts around 1-2 weeks<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
      • paroxysmal phase
        • the cough increases in severity<\/li>
        • coughing bouts are usually worse at night and after feeding, may be ended by vomiting & associated central cyanosis<\/li>
        • inspiratory whoop: not always present (caused by forced inspiration against a closed glottis)<\/li>
        • infants may have spells of apnoea<\/span><\/li>
        • persistent coughing may cause subconjunctival haemorrhages or even anoxia leading to syncope & seizures<\/li>
        • lasts between 2-8 weeks<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
        • convalescent phase
          • the cough subsides over weeks to months<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
            Diagnostic criteria<\/b>
            Whooping cough should be suspected if a person has an acute cough that has lasted for 14 days or more without another apparent cause, and has one or more of the following features:
            • Paroxysmal cough.<\/li>
            • Inspiratory whoop.<\/li>
            • Post-tussive vomiting.<\/li>
            • Undiagnosed apnoeic attacks in young infants.<\/li><\/ul><\/div>
              Diagnosis
              • per nasal swab culture for Bordetella pertussis<\/i> - may take several days or weeks to come back<\/li>
              • PCR and serology are now increasingly used as their availability becomes more widespread<\/li><\/ul>
                Management
                • infants under 6 months with suspect pertussis should be admitted<\/li>
                • in the UK pertussis is a notifiable<\/span> disease<\/li>
                • an oral macrolide<\/span> (e.g. clarithromycin, azithromycin or erythromycin) is indicated if the onset of the cough is within the previous 21 days<\/span> to eradicate the organism and reduce the spread<\/li>
                • household contacts should be offered antibiotic prophylaxis<\/li>
                • antibiotic therapy has not been shown to alter the course of the illness<\/li>
                • school exclusion: 48 hours<\/span> after commencing antibiotics (or 21 days from onset of symptoms if no antibiotics )<\/li><\/ul>
                  Complications
                  • subconjunctival haemorrhage<\/li>

                  • seizures<\/li><\/ul>
                    Vaccination of pregnant women<\/b><\/span>

                    In 2012 there was an outbreak of whooping cough (pertussis) which resulted in the death of 14 newborn children. As a temporary measure, a vaccination programme was introduced in 2012 for pregnant women. This has successfully reduced the number of cases of whooping cough (the vaccine is thought to be more than 90% effective in preventing newborns developing whooping cough). It was however decided in 2014 to extend the whooping cough vaccination programme for pregnant women. This decision was taken as there was a 'great deal of uncertainty' about the timing of future outbreaks.

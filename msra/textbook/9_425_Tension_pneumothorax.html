          Pathophysiology
          • tension pneumothorax develops when air enters the pleural space and is unable to escape, creating a one-way valve effect<\/li>
          • this can be due to a flap of tissue acting as a valve, or a similar mechanism in a traumatic or iatrogenic wound<\/li>
          • the continuous accumulation of air leads to increased intrapleural pressure which exceeds atmospheric pressure throughout the respiratory cycle<\/li>
          • as intrapleural pressure rises, it exerts several deleterious effects:

            • mediastinal shift: Increased pressure pushes the mediastinum towards the contralateral side. This shift can compress the opposite lung, further impairing respiratory function. Tracheal deviation, although a late and ominous sign, is a key diagnostic indicator<\/li>

            • reduced cardiac filling: The direct pressure on the heart, coupled with reduced venous return, impairs both right and left ventricular filling. This can precipitantly lower cardiac output and systemic blood pressure<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>

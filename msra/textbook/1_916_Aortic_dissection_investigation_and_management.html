Classification<\/h5>
Stanford classification
  • type A - ascending aorta, 2\/3 of cases<\/li>
  • type B - descending aorta, distal to left subclavian origin, 1\/3 of cases<\/li><\/ul>
    DeBakey classification
    • type I - originates in ascending aorta, propagates to at least the aortic arch and possibly beyond it distally<\/li>
    • type II - originates in and is confined to the ascending aorta<\/li>
    • type III - originates in descending aorta, rarely extends proximally but will extend distally<\/li><\/ul>
      Investigation<\/h5>
      It's important to remember that patients may present acutely and be clinically unstable. The choice of investigations will have to take account of this.

      Chest x-ray
      • widened mediastinum<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
        CT angiography<\/span> of the chest, abdomen and pelvis is the investigation of choice
        • suitable for stable patients and for planning surgery<\/li>
        • a false lumen<\/span> is a key finding in diagnosing aortic dissection <\/li><\/ul>
          Transoesophageal echocardiography (TOE)<\/span>
          • more suitable for unstable patients who are too risky to take to CT scanner<\/li><\/ul>
            Management<\/h5>
            Type A
            • surgical management, but blood pressure should be controlled to a target systolic of 100-120 mmHg whilst awaiting intervention<\/li><\/ul>
              Type B*
              • conservative management<\/li>
              • bed rest<\/li>
              • reduce blood pressure IV labetalol to prevent progression<\/li><\/ul>
                Complications<\/h5>
                Complications of backward tear
                • aortic incompetence\/regurgitation<\/li>
                • MI: inferior pattern is often seen due to right coronary involvement<\/li><\/ul>
                  Complications of a forward tear
                  • unequal arm pulses and BP<\/li>
                  • stroke<\/li>
                  • renal failure<\/li><\/ul>

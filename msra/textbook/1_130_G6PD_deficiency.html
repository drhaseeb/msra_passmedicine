[{"notes_id":"1_130","category":"5","subcategory":"2","title":"G6PD deficiency","body":"Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency is the commonest red blood cell enzyme defect. It is more common in people from the Mediterranean and Africa and is inherited in an X-linked recessive fashion. Many drugs can precipitate a crisis as well as infections and broad (fava) beans

Pathophysiology
  • G6PD is the first step in the pentose phosphate pathway, which converts glucose-6-phosphate→ 6-phosphogluconolactone
    • this reaction also results in nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADP) → NADPH<\/li>
    • i.e. glucose-6-phosphate + NADP → 6-phosphogluconolactone + NADPH<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
    • NADPH is important for converting oxidizied glutathione back to it's reduced form<\/li>
    • reduced glutathione protects red blood cells from oxidative damage by oxidants such as superoxide anion (O2-) and hydrogen peroxide<\/li>
    • ↓ G6PD → ↓ reduced NADPH → ↓ reduced glutathione → increased red cell susceptibility to oxidative stress<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
      Features<\/span>
      • neonatal jaundice is often seen<\/li>
      • intravascular haemolysis<\/li>
      • gallstones are common<\/li>
      • splenomegaly may be present<\/li>
      • Heinz bodies<\/span> on blood films. Bite and blister cells<\/span> may also be seen<\/li><\/ul>
        Diagnosis is made by using a G6PD enzyme assay
        • levels should be checked around 3 months after an acute episode of hemolysis<\/span>, RBCs with the most severely reduced G6PD activity will have hemolysed → reduced G6PD activity → not be measured in the assay → false negative results<\/li><\/ul>
          Some drugs causing haemolysis
          • anti-malarials: primaquine<\/span><\/li>
          • ciprofloxacin<\/span><\/li>
          • sulph- group drugs: sulphonamides, sulphasalazine, sulfonylureas<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
            Some drugs thought to be safe
            • penicillins<\/li>
            • cephalosporins<\/li>
            • macrolides<\/li>
            • tetracyclines<\/li>
            • trimethoprim<\/li><\/ul>

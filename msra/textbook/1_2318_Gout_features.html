Features<\/h5>
Gout is a form of inflammatory arthritis. Patients typically have episodes lasting several days when their gout flares and are often symptom-free between episodes. The acute episodes typically develop maximal intensity with 12 hours. The main features it presents with are:
  • pain: this is often very significant<\/li>
  • swelling<\/li>
  • erythema<\/li><\/ul>
    Around 70% of first presentations affect the 1st metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint<\/span>. Attacks of gout affecting this area were historically called podagra. Other commonly affected joints include:
    • ankle<\/li>
    • wrist<\/li>
    • knee<\/li><\/ul>
      If untreated repeated acute episodes of gout can damage the joints resulting in a more chronic joint problem.

      Investigations<\/h5>
      Uric acid
      • NICE recommends measuring uric acid levels in suspected gout (i.e. in the acute setting)<\/span>
        • a uric acid level \u2265 360 umol\/L is seen as supporting a diagnosis<\/li>
        • if uric acid level < 360 umol\/L during a flare and gout is strongly suspected, repeat the uric acid level measurement at least 2 weeks after the flare has settled<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
          Synovial fluid analysis
          • needle shaped negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals under polarised light<\/span><\/li><\/ul>
            Radiological features of gout include:
            • joint effusion is an early sign<\/li>
            • well-defined 'punched-out' erosions<\/span> with sclerotic margins in a juxta-articular distribution, often with overhanging edges<\/li>
            • relative preservation of joint space until late disease<\/li>
            • eccentric erosions<\/li>
            • no periarticular osteopenia (in contrast to rheumatoid arthritis)<\/li>
            • soft tissue tophi may be seen<\/li><\/ul>

So which features make pulmonary embolism more likely?<\/h5>
The PIOPED study1<\/sup> in 2007 looked at the frequency of different symptoms and signs in patients who were diagnosed with pulmonary embolism.

The relative frequency of common clinical signs is shown below:
  • Tachypnea (respiratory rate >20\/min) - 96%<\/li>
  • Crackles - 58%<\/li>
  • Tachycardia<\/span> (heart rate >100\/min) - 44%<\/li>
  • Fever (temperature >37.8\u00b0C) - 43%<\/li><\/ul>
    It is interesting to note that the Well's criteria for diagnosing a PE use tachycardia rather than tachypnoea.

    All patients with symptoms or signs suggestive of a PE should have a history taken, examination performed and a chest x-ray to exclude other pathology.


    Pulmonary embolism rule-out criteria (PERC)<\/h5>
    NICE updated their guidelines on the investigation and management of venous thromboembolism (VTE) in 2020. One of the key changes was the use of the pulmonary embolism rule-out criteria (the PERC<\/b> rule)
    • a copy of criteria can be found in the image below<\/li>
    • all<\/b> the criteria must be absent<\/span> to have negative PERC result, i.e. rule-out PE<\/li>
    • this should be done when you think there is a low pre-test probability of PE<\/span>, but want more reassurance that it isn't the diagnosis
      • this low probability is defined as < 15%<\/span>, although it is clearly difficult to quantify such judgements<\/li>
      • NICE state 'If clinical suspicion of PE is low based on the overall clinical impression (from general medical history, physical examination and any initial investigations such as electrocardiography or chest X-ray), and other diagnoses are feasible<\/i>' - a possible example of this would be a low-risk patient with very typical musculoskeletal chest pain<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
      • a negative PERC reduces the probability of PE to < 2%<\/span> <\/li>
      • if your suspicion of PE is greater than this then you should move straight to the 2-level PE Wells score, without doing a PERC<\/li><\/ul>

                  CTPA or V\/Q scan?<\/h5>
                  The consensus view from the British Thoracic Society and NICE guidelines is as follows:
                  • CTPA is now the recommended initial lung-imaging modality for non-massive PE<\/span>. Advantages compared to V\/Q scans include speed, easier to perform out-of-hours, a reduced need for further imaging and the possibility of providing an alternative diagnosis if PE is excluded <\/li>
                  • if the CTPA is negative then patients do not need further investigations or treatment for PE<\/li>
                  • V\/Q scanning may be used initially if appropriate facilities exist, the chest x-ray is normal, and there is no significant symptomatic concurrent cardiopulmonary disease. V\/Q scanning is also the investigation of choice if there is renal impairment<\/span> (doesn't require the use of contrast unlike CTPA)<\/li><\/ul>

                    Some other points<\/h5>
                    D-dimers
                    • sensitivity = 95-98%, but poor specificity<\/li>
                    • age-adjusted D-dimer levels should be considered for patients > 50 years<\/li><\/ul>
                      ECG
                      • the classic ECG changes seen in PE are a large S wave in lead I, a large Q wave in lead III and an inverted T wave in lead III - 'S1Q3T3<\/span>'. However, this change is seen in no more than 20% of patients<\/li>
                      • right bundle branch block and right axis deviation are also associated with PE<\/li>
                      • sinus tachycardia<\/span> may also be seen<\/li><\/ul>

                        Chest x-ray
                        • a chest x-ray is recommended for all patients<\/span> to exclude other pathology<\/li>

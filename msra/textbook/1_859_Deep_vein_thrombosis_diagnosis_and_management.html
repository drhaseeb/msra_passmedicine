                    Management<\/h5>
                    The cornerstone of VTE management is anticoagulant therapy. This was historically done with warfarin, often preceded by heparin until the INR was stable. However, the development of DOACs, and an evidence base supporting their efficacy, has changed modern management.

                    Choice of anticoagulant
                    • the big change in the 2020 guidelines was the increased use of DOACs<\/li>
                    • apixaban or rivaroxaban (both DOACs) should be offered first-line following the diagnosis of a DVT
                      • instead of using low-molecular weight heparin (LMWH) until the diagnosis is confirmed, NICE now advocate using a DOAC once a diagnosis is suspected<\/span>, with this continued if the diagnosis is confirmed<\/li>
                      • if neither apixaban or rivaroxaban are suitable then either LMWH followed by dabigatran or edoxaban OR LMWH followed by a vitamin K antagonist (VKA, i.e. warfarin)<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
                      • if the patient has active cancer
                        • previously LMWH was recommended<\/li>
                        • the new guidelines now recommend using a DOAC<\/span>, unless this is contraindicated<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
                        • if renal impairment is severe (e.g. < 15\/min) then LMWH, unfractionated heparin or LMWH followed by a VKA<\/li>
                        • if the patient has antiphospholipid syndrome (specifically 'triple positive' in the guidance) then LMWH followed by a VKA should be used<\/li><\/ul>
                          Length of anticoagulation
                          • all patients should have anticoagulation for at least 3 months<\/li>
                          • continuing anticoagulation after this period is partly determined by whether the VTE was provoked or unprovoked
                            • a provoked VTE is due to an obvious precipitating event e.g. immobilisation following major surgery. The implication is that this event was transient and the patient is no longer at increased risk<\/li>
                            • an unprovoked VTE occurs in the absence of an obvious precipitating event, i.e. there is a possibility that there are unknown factors (e.g. mild thrombophilia) making the patient more at risk from further clots<\/li><\/ul><\/li>
                            • if the VTE was provoked the treatment is typically stopped after the initial 3 months<\/span> (3 to 6 months for people with active cancer)<\/li>

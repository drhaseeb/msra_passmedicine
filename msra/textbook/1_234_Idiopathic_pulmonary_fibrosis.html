Whilst there are many causes of lung fibrosis (e.g. medications, connective tissue disease, asbestos) the term IPF is reserved when no underlying cause exists.

IPF is typically seen in patients aged 50-70 years and is twice as common in men.

  • progressive exertional dyspnoea<\/li>
  • bibasal fine end-inspiratory crepitations on auscultation<\/span><\/li>

    Diagnosis
    • spirometry: classically a restrictive picture (FEV1 normal\/decreased, FVC decreased, FEV1\/FVC increased)<\/span><\/li>
    • impaired gas exchange: reduced transfer factor (TLCO)<\/li>
    • imaging: bilateral interstitial shadowing (typically small, irregular, peripheral opacities - 'ground-glass' - later progressing to 'honeycombing') may be seen on a chest x-ray but high-resolution CT scanning is the investigation of choice<\/span> and required to make a diagnosis of IPF<\/li>
    • ANA positive in 30%, rheumatoid factor positive in 10% but this does not necessarily mean that the fibrosis is secondary to a connective tissue disease. Titres are usually low<\/li><\/ul>
      Management
      • pulmonary rehabilitation<\/li>
      • very few medications have been shown to give any benefit in IPF. There is some evidence that pirfenidone (an antifibrotic agent) may be useful in selected patients<\/span> (see NICE guidelines)<\/li>
      • many patients will require supplementary oxygen and eventually a lung transplant<\/li><\/ul>
        Prognosis
        • poor, average life expectancy is around 3-4 years<\/li><\/ul>

[{"notes_id":"1_164","category":"4","subcategory":"1","title":"Coeliac disease: investigation","body":"Coeliac disease is caused by sensitivity to the protein gluten. Repeated exposure leads to villous atrophy which in turn causes malabsorption. Conditions associated with coeliac disease include dermatitis herpetiformis (a vesicular, pruritic skin eruption) and autoimmune disorders (type 1 diabetes mellitus and autoimmune hepatitis).

Diagnosis is made by a combination of serology and endoscopic intestinal biopsy. Villous atrophy and immunology normally reverses on a gluten-free diet.

NICE issued guidelines on the investigation of coeliac disease in 2009. If patients are already taking a gluten-free diet they should be asked, if possible, to reintroduce gluten for at least 6 weeks prior to testing<\/span>.

Serology
  • tissue transglutaminase (TTG) antibodies (IgA) are first-choice<\/span> according to NICE<\/li>
  • endomyseal antibody (IgA)<\/span>
    • needed to look for selective IgA deficiency, which would give a false negative coeliac result<\/li>
    • if IgA deficiency test for IgG endomyseal antibodies, IgG deamidated gliadin peptide (DGP) or IgG transglutaminase antibodies<\/span><\/li><\/ul><\/li>
    • anti-gliadin antibody (IgA or IgG) tests are not recommended by NICE<\/li>
    • anti-casein antibodies are also found in some patients<\/li><\/ul>
      Endoscopic intestinal biopsy<\/span>
      • the 'gold standard' for diagnosis - this should be performed in all patients with suspected coeliac disease to confirm or exclude the diagnosis<\/li>
      • traditionally done in the duodenum but jejunal biopsies are also sometimes performed<\/li>
      • findings supportive of coeliac disease<\/span>:
        • villous atrophy<\/li>
        • crypt hyperplasia<\/li>
        • increase in intraepithelial lymphocytes<\/li>
        • lamina propria infiltration with lymphocytes<\/li><\/ul><\/li><\/ul>
          Rectal gluten challenge has been described but is not widely used

The guidance covers what constitutes an intimate examination, how a doctor should conduct such an examination and how chaperones should be involved in the process.

Intimate examinations:
  • Essentially this is anything the patient would consider intimate or intrusive<\/li>
  • This will most commonly be examinations of the rectum, genitalia and breasts<\/li>
  • This definition can be different for any patient and you should think about whether the patient would consider what you are doing intimate<\/li><\/ul>
    Performing the examination:
    • As with any procedure, you should seek informed consent from the patient to carry out the examination<\/li>
    • This involves explaining what you will do and why and how exposed they will be<\/li>
    • You should make sure they are happy to proceed and ensure they have had all their questions answered<\/li>
    • In addition you should explain that you will stop the examination at any point if asked to by the patient<\/li>
    • During the examination it is good practice to only talk if it is relevant to the examination<\/li><\/ul>
      Chaperones:
      • A chaperone is an impartial person who is present during the examination to both offer support to the patient and act as an observer to ensure the examination is carried out in a professional manner<\/li>
      • They should be a healthcare worker who has no relation to the patient or doctor. Patients may also wish to have family members present for support but they cannot be relied upon as chaperones as they are not impartial<\/li>
      • The full name and role of the chaperone should be documented in the medical records<\/span> with the examination findings<\/li>
      • In the event of the patient making any allegations against the doctor with regards to the examination, the chaperone can be called on as a witness<\/li>
      • The chaperone should also be able to safely announce whether they feel any behaviours are inappropriate during the examination<\/li>
      • It is not mandatory to have a chaperone and many patients may wish to be examined without another individual present. In these cases, the offer and refusal should be documented in the medical records<\/span><\/li>
      • A doctor should not feel pressured to perform an examination without a chaperone if they do not wish. However, they should ensure the patient is referred to a colleague who is comfortable doing so and that they would not wait unnecessarily<\/span> for treatment because of this<\/li><\/ul>

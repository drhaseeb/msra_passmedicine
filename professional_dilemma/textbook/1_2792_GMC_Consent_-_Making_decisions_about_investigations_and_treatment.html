When referring to treatment, not just invasive or dangerous procedures such as surgery are taken into account but all forms of treatment including changes to medication or how to investigate a condition. The guidance explains how and what information should be shared with patients to gain consent, who can make decisions on their behalf if a patient cannot and how consent is expressed and recorded.

Providing information:
  • You should explain all the available treatment options to the patient and the benefits and risks of each treatment<\/li>
  • If you think a treatment is more appropriate you should make a recommendation but ensure you do not bias the patient or coerce them into choosing your preference for treatment<\/li>
  • When discussing the risks of a treatment you should provide information regarding any risks which would be relevant to the patient; this may be both because they are serious but rare or because they are common but less significant<\/li>
  • You are obliged to ensure the patient is aware of the risks of a procedure unless they have specifically asked not to be informed; do not assume they would not want to know<\/li>
  • Even when patients request that information is withheld, you must still ensure that they are aware of the risks associated with any treatment so consent can be obtained<\/li>
  • You can only withhold information when it would cause 'significant harm' to the patient when giving it; there needs to be evidence, not just a doctor's feeling that it would upset or dissuade a patient<\/li>
  • You should answer any questions honestly and as fully as you can, even if you think this will result in them withdrawing consent for a procedure<\/li><\/ul>
    Explaining risks:
    • The discussion should be tailored to the individual and risks adjusted based on their co-morbidities and personal background as appropriate<\/li>
    • You must always explain side-effects and possible complications of a treatment as well as the risk of failure of treatment<\/li><\/ul>
      Obtaining and recording consent:
      • You must document in the medical records that a patient has consented to a treatment based on the information given to them; you must record the benefits and risks you have explained<\/li>
      • This must be done both for trivial treatments such as a new oral medication as well as more significant treatments such as surgical procedures<\/li>
      • Written consent should be obtained for procedures with significant risk or significant impact on the patient's social situation to formally document the conversation regarding benefits and risks. This should be in addition to recording in the medical records that they have given consent<\/li>
      • You are legally required to gain written consent for fertility treatment<\/li>
      • Where possible, the patient should be provided with written information regarding the risks and benefits discussed if the treatment is significant; this may include a copy of a consent form or clinic letter or a leaflet with further information<\/li>
      • In the case of patients under 16, consent must be sought from the parents and their written consent must be documented<\/li><\/ul>
        Non-competent patients:
        • Any patient who cannot consent for themselves, either due to a reduced conscious level or a cognitive impairment, can still have treatment in their best interests<\/span><\/li>
        • The reasons for carrying out the procedure must be clearly documented<\/li><\/ul>

          • The benefits and reasons for the procedure, the risks of the procedure itself, possible post-operative complications and the possibility of failure of treatment should all be discussed and documented<\/li><\/ul>

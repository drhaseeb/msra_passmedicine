The GMC highlight the importance of a confidential medical service. However, sometimes patients are unable to protect themselves, and we may need to disclose personal information to protect their health and welfare.

I'm worried that my patient is at risk of harm abuse or neglect- Should I disclose?<\/h5>
1) Empower patients- make decisions about how to best move forward in partnership

2) If disclosure is required by law- You must disclose, but only the specific requested information, in the manner required by law. Inform your patient of disclosure (unless It would undermine the purpose)

3) Some disclosures are permitted but not required by law- these should have a legal basis

4) How you act will partly depend on whether this patient has capacity or lacks capacity:

Can I share information to protect others\/the public?<\/h5>
Confidentiality is in the public interest as it benefits society and individuals to seek medical care. However, this must be balanced with protecting and promoting the health of patients and the public.

    Think about the nature of the information and what the patient thinks. Can you minimise the breach of the patient's privacy and still avoid harm?

    Genetic medicine introduces another hurdle in relation to confidentiality, because if your patient Is found to have a genetic condition, this might also suggest a probability of this condition in a relative. Many patients share these results with relatives especially if they know it can help to prevent health problems in their relatives. However, if a patient refuses, it would be just to disclose if non-disclosure would risk death or serious harm in others. Try to not disclose patient identity when advising the others of the risks identified to them.

    Always document in patient records when disclosing information. Document steps you've taken to seek consent from or inform the patient about the disclosure, or reasons for not doing so.

    Whether you can disclose without consent can be complex. Before doing so, seek advice from a Caldicott or data guardian who isn't connected to the reason for the disclosure.
